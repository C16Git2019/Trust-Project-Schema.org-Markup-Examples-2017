My trip to a 2,000-year-old neighbourhood

By Vawn Himmelsbach
Tues., Sept. 18, 2018

I’m not sure about the bean curd pudding, though Hong-Kongers consider this “classic” comfort food. So I sprinkle some raw cane sugar on it, as is the custom, and give it a try, along with freshly made soy milk and fried tofu puffs. And I start to understand all this fuss over bean curd.

Here, at Kung Wo Bean Curd Factory, tofu is still made the traditional way by soaking dried soybeans in water for up to 10 hours, then using a granite mill to gently extract the milk. And this particular factory, with a small restaurant and shop, has been an institution in the working-class neighbourhood of Sham Shui Po since 1967.

All the work is done by hand, rather than by machines (taking a peek in the back, I see how laborious the process is). Today, there are fewer than 10 bean curd factories left in Hong Kong that make tofu by hand, explains Renee So, who runs the factory with her father, Sung-lim. The result, however, is a silky, smooth texture. So silky and smooth, in fact, it’s recommended in Michelin’s street food guide.

Authentic food experiences — which includes Michelin-starred dim sum at Tim Ho Wan — is just one reason to visit Sham Shui Po. Barely changed since the 1960s, this neighbourhood is one of Hong Kong’s oldest districts, located in the heart of Kowloon, home to 400,000 people in a 20-square-kilometre radius.

This neighbourhood has been inhabited for about 2,000 years, though it experienced a boom in the 1960s when it became the centre of Hong Kong’s wholesale and manufacturing textile business. Though that business eventually moved to Mainland China, the textile market remains. Ki Lung Street sells fabric, both wholesale and retail, while Nam Cheong Street, Yu Chau Street and Ki Lung Street offer ribbons, beads and buttons. There’s also a vast electronics market, the Apliu Street Flea, with new and second-hand electronic equipment.

Alongside traditional businesses, young artists and designers are turning historic buildings — untouched from the 1930s — into revitalized spaces. I visit a traditional paper-crafting business, where the young proprietor makes Taoist paper effigies for funerals or other festivals. A few doors down, I stop for a pour-over coffee at an uber-trendy hipster café with a maker space in the back.

Sham Shui Po is a neighbourhood in transition, but one that still offers a taste of authentic Hong Kong with its local markets, iconic signboards and street-food vendors. This has prompted a new campaign by the Hong Kong Tourism Board to promote the neighbourhood with Explore Hong Kong Tours, featuring history, food, art and culture.

Also worth a visit (or stay) is the revitalized Mei Ho House, one of the Shek Kip Mei resettlement blocks built in 1954, which marked the beginning of Hong Kong’s public housing programme. Mei Ho House is the only surviving Mark I H-shaped resettlement block in Hong Kong, which has been turned into a heritage museum and youth hostel, with nostalgic-themed rooms and retro-styled café.

To get here, take the MRT to Tuen Wan or Tung Chung; the main sights are all within walking distance. For more information, visit discoverhongkong.com.
